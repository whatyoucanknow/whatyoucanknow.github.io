Background of Prison and Work

Before exploring why prison labor is a fruitful opportunity, one needs to understand America’s prison system. Criminal punishment has five possible purposes: incapacitation, deterrence, retribution, rehabilitation, and restitution. Incapacitation removes the defendant from society, deterrence frightens the defendant or the public of punishment, retribution punishes the defendant, restitution satisfies the defendant’s debt to society, and rehabilitation alters the defendant’s behavior. All of these purposes combine to accomplish one goal: prevent crime (The Purposes of Punishment). Although the equal use of these goals may be ideal, some tend to outweigh others in American prisons. The prison system becomes corrupt when retribution and incapacitation dominate prison culture. Incapacitation keeps crime away from the public but does not decrease the prisoner’s desire to commit a crime. Incapacitation only increases crime inside the prison and increases the prisoner’s desire to get even with society (Leopold). Retribution justifies inhumane and unacceptable practices in prison such as bad food, rough guards, and hostile living situations. Rehabilitation is lacking in the current system and needs to be a priority. America’s focus on retribution and incapacitation to achieve the goal of preventing crime needs to change because they promote hostile situations inside prisons and are not effective in reducing the number of criminals. In America, there are more people imprisoned and more money being spent on prisons than ever before in world history. This is mass incarceration, a cyclical system in which more people are going to prison. This is caused by a system where prison time is easily sentenced and prisoners can not re-enter society due to an ineffective prison system. In 1972, there were less than 200,000 prisoners, and now, there are more than 2.2 million. The United States holds only 5 percent of the world’s population, but 25 percent of its prisoners. With this amount of prisoners, America spends $87 billion on prisons per year (“Mass Incarceration”). These statistics show that there are too many people in prison, creating a serious problem that needs to be addressed. Mass incarceration is expedited by recidivism, the tendency of a convicted criminal to reoffend (“Recidivism”). In a study done on 404,638 state prisoners released in 2005 across 30 states, 30.4 percent returned to prison in the year after release. After 2 years, that number grew to 43.3 percent and at 5 years it is 55.1 percent. That means that upon release, a prisoner has a 50 percent chance of going back to prison (Durose). Our current prison system is unable to prevent further crime from the majority of prisoners. The system is not working to prevent crime or rehabilitate the prisoner and the long period that a prisoner spends in prison becomes meaningless when the majority return to prison within five years. The problem of recidivism both accelerates mass incarceration and makes prison pointless in its current form when the ultimate goal is to prevent crime.

Retribution and incapacitation mindsets in American prisons foster recidivism and mass incarceration. How should America combat mass incarceration and recidivism? How does America create a system where a prisoner is released and does not reoffend? Redefining prison from retribution and incapacitation to rehabilitation is key. Most prisons have rehabilitation programs through education, counseling, or work. These programs are not implemented across America and are specific to each prison. Currently, most prisoners have the option to hold a job in prison but are never required. Their work could be fulfilled by a work release program where they are employed outside of the prison, work inside the prison where they clean or cook, or with companies that come to prisons for workers. Is prison labor a reasonable tactic to prevent mass incarceration and recidivism? What effect is this work having on a prisoner?

With the dangerous problems of mass incarceration and recidivism today, change must be made. We need rehabilitation of prisoners to combat these problems, rather than incapacitation and retribution that continues the cycle of stripping away the sense of humanity and increasing the poor treatment of prisoners. Some may argue that the solution is to reduce prison sentences. Others may argue that prisons should educate prisoners and provide therapists. Some argue that we should make prisoners work to give money back to the country, others say to make them work to promote real-world habits for an easier transition back into society.

Work is one of the essential factors for a functioning society. For a prolonged time, men and women have been working in exchange for payment. The first humans worked on the land to put food on the table, most parents work for money, and students work at school for an education. Governments and societies have exploited this exchange throughout history as seen in the gender wage gap, child labor, minimum wage, and slavery. Slavery is when individuals are owned by others. The owners can control where the slaves live and what they do. A slave is seen as property and is forced to work for nothing. Slavery has a long history, from the ancient Greeks to pre-civil war America (What Is Slavery?). Prison labor, often forced labor, is when prisoners are required to work or hold a job in prison. It is important that as a country we can define what prison labor is, so it stays away from corruption and does not become slavery. Prison labor can be separated from slavery by paying prisoners, applying labor laws similar to those in our current workforce, holding an American-wide standard for prison labor, and seeing prisoners as employees rather than property. But why is work so important? What is the purpose? Many would say that work moves the economy, gives people a purpose, and is unavoidable.

When looking at the issues of the current prison system, the bigger question we face is human progress and treating humans with dignity. How should the government set its citizens up for success in the world? Currently, prisoners feel degraded by the prison system and life after prison seems impossible, creating a path for prisoners to reoffend and reinforcing habits that make it more likely for them to reoffend. Prison should be a place for rehabilitation, not incapacitation that increases violence. Chandra Bozelko, a Princeton University graduate, is a criminal justice reform author and was formerly incarcerated where she participated in the prison labor system. She has been published on major publications and her blog, recognized as an expert on incarceration. Bozelko realized that “the reason why prisoners are getting in trouble once they are already in trouble in prison is they were not making use of their time, they were bored” (Bozelko). Prisoners are sitting idle in prison. Rather than using their time in prison for rehabilitation, the current system is forcing them to sit in their cells away from society and work. In 1982, the U.S. General Accounting Office realized the issue of prisoner idleness and conducted a study of prison work programs to decrease this idleness, or laziness. The study stated that idleness creates “destructive behavior” and “does little to prepare inmates for reentry into society”, so it is something that has to be decreased before a prisoner reenters society (The United States General Accounting Office). The U.S. government’s answer to ineffective prisons was work. In the study, they came to the overall conclusion that work was good for the prisoner; the prisoner was able to exchange work for a better prison experience and a better life after prison. We need to realize work is the solution and take action on these current issues. The requirement of prison labor is essential to rehabilitation of the prisoner and the decrease of mass incarceration and recidivism. Concerning the issue of whether prisoners should be required to work in public prisons, one must first ask these questions: How does prison labor reform the prisoner? How does prison labor affect the prisoner’s emotional health? Should prisoners be paid for their work?

Effects of Prison Labor

By looking at current implementations of prison labor, one can see that prison labor teaches work ethic and sets the prisoner up for success upon release, reducing recidivism and increasing the employment rate. Prison labor builds work ethic by creating real-world scenarios that parallel work outside of prison such as dealing with a boss, experiencing promotion for a job well done, working alongside co-workers, or learning to adapt skills. Chandra Bozelko testifies that most of the prisoners she saw around her “didn’t know how to work hard.” She makes it clear that they were not lazy, “they just were never told if you go the extra mile, you might get something extra.” When Bozelko’s work crew received a special meal for getting their job done quickly, her coworkers “were constantly shocked, not at the kindness, but that they were rewarded for working hard.” Some of these prisoners had never been taught that hard, timely, and quality work can be rewarded with a bonus, overtime pay rates, or good standing with a boss. Bozelko proclaims, “this concept was introduced to them and I saw it change them” (Bozelko). Bozelko witnessed and explained the effect of prison labor that statistics agree with; she witnessed prisoners learning what work means through experience. Many of us take rewards that work produces as common knowledge, when many people do not know what that means.

With the knowledge of work ethic and work experience, prisoners will be able to get jobs and keep jobs after prison. This is shown by a study conducted by the Urban Institute Justice Policy Center on 740 ethnically and racially diverse former male prisoners in Illinois, Ohio, and Texas. They found that “respondents who held a prison job and those who participated in job-training programs while incarcerated had better employment outcomes after release.” Those who participated in job-training had a 62 percent chance of getting a job after prison compared with 41 percent for those who did not participate. Not only did they find a job easier, but many “were interested in participating in community programs to further improve their work skills” (Employment). Working in prison made the employment process smoother and quicker and shaped the prisoner to want to work and learn in their community. Requiring prisoners to work will set prisoners up for success after prison. The Washington State Department of Corrections conducted a report on a group of offenders with similar demographic and offense characteristics. Half had participated in prison labor and the other half had not. The report showed that three years after release, 34.4 percent of the prisoners who participated in prison labor were employed and only 8.2 percent of the prisoners who did not participate were employed (Evans). Although the rate of employment is still low, it is significantly higher when the prisoner participates in prison labor.

Looking at the bigger picture, prison labor reduces recidivism and therefore reduces mass incarceration because prisoners who can hold jobs after prison have less reason to reoffend. In that same Washington State Department of Corrections report, not only were employment rates drastically higher after participating in prison labor, but recidivism rates lowered. The report showed that three years after release, only 47.2 percent of the prisoners who participated in prison labor reoffended and 61.4 percent of the prisoners who did not participate reoffended (Evans). Prison labor has a direct, positive relationship with the increase in employment and a decrease in recidivism after being released. Working to increase employment and decrease recidivism even more across the whole country can be supported by all public prisons requiring prisoners to participate in work programs. Through more research, these numbers can change even more by improving work programs and increasing participation.

Prison Labor's Effect on Emotional Health

Prison labor will restore the prisoner’s sense of humanity and improve the prisoner’s emotional health. The current prison system strips away a prisoner’s sense of humanity because of their focus on incapacitation and retribution, but prison labor helps to restore that sense of humanity by focusing on reformation. Jesse Luke Crosson, an inmate in Buckingham Correctional Center serving a 32-year sentence, participates in a work program. He writes, “It feels special to handle hammer-drills and side-cutters in a place where a paper clip is contraband. There is a trust in my person and in my competence, which gives me validation”. The simple act of giving a prisoner tools or a task shows that he can be trusted and have a purpose. While banning paper clips is dehumanizing, allowing the use of tools brings back the prisoners’ sense of humanity. Crosson makes the point that these jobs are not pointless; they have a function in society: “A group of unemployable cons somehow joins together, usually without training or instruction, and keeps the lights on” (Crosson). This work is giving them a role in society and their community that produces a reward. The feeling of having a role and purpose in society restores the humanity of a prisoner as someone who’s life holds worth. Keeping the humanity of prisoners intact is important because it gives them hope for their future.

Restoring a prisoner’s sense of humanity and giving him purpose works to improve mental health. The Pennsylvania Department of Corrections created Pennsylvania Correctional Industries (PCI) to rehabilitate inmates through employment, vocational training, and work experience. Inmates participating in this program “took much pride in their work and saw it as a responsibility that held great meaning for them” and even said it “can be beneficial to their mental well-being—their work instilling feelings of societal contribution and a metaphorical ‘freedom’ from prison” (Littletree-Holston). By working with employers who want the job done well, inmates learn how to work well because staff would not give up on them when they did something wrong, “giving inmates the feelings of pride and accomplishment conducive to a healthier mental state” (Littletree-Holston). It is important to not only emphasize success after prison but also mental health after prison. To successfully reduce recidivism and get prisoners to want to turn their life around, it is important to improve mental health. In a study of 70 inmates working in this Pennsylvania program, the dominant perception of the prison labor was that it “was a beneficial aspect of prison including improvement of self-perception, incentive to avoid conflict, enhancement of interpersonal skills, and job training/education” (Littletree-Holston). Through rehabilitation, this work program was able to give the prisoners purpose, connection to the outside world, and a version of normalcy while simultaneously teaching them.

Why is Prison Labor the Best Solution

While some may not be opposed to requiring prisoners to work, they may see other reform programs, such as education or counseling, as better tactics in reducing recidivism and mass incarceration. At first glance, reform programs based on education and counseling seem to be an option to reform prisoners. Education could give prisoners an education they may have missed out on and prepare them for future careers upon release. Counseling could discover why a prisoner committed a crime and reform their worldview to not want to commit a crime again. While these are valid proposals, prison labor integrates both education and counseling. As mentioned before, prison labor educates prisoners about work ethic, relations with bosses and co-workers, job interviews, and the necessities to be a successful employee and human in society. Work engages prisoners in real-life experiences, rather than learning through discussion, lectures, or activities other programs may offer that don’t allow inmates to experience the real-world. Also mentioned before, prison labor restores a prisoner’s sense of purpose, hope, and humanity, the factors counseling often targets. While education and counseling alone will be unable to fully target a prisoner’s work ethic and preparedness for life after prison, prison labor can along with addressing mental health and necessary education.

Prisoner Wages

To enforce required prison labor throughout public prisons in America, there needs to be a separate minimum wage for prisoners. Prisoner minimum wage should be less than the regular minimum wage, but this number will make sure prisoners are being paid for their labor. Low wages for working prisoners are necessary to entice the government or other company programs to employ prisoners. If companies and the government were required to pay prisoners minimum wage, then they would rather hire someone not in prison for minimum wage. In an interview, Chandra Bozelko says, “low wages are unfair, but they are the grease that gets this whole part of the system going” (Bozelko). She admits that she would have loved to make more money, but recognizes that the only way to get money and experience is through low wages. The outside companies will be able to hire prisoners for less, giving the prisoner the positive experience of work while simultaneously saving the company money. Although allowing outside companies to hire inmates at a lower cost is concerning due to the threat of slave labor and corruption, they are necessary to allow each prisoner to have a job, as there will not be enough jobs inside the prison, and to allow the prisoner to make connections with real companies. Ample laws and attention must be drawn to companies hiring prisoners to prevent corruption. Normalizing prison labor and working out the problems is what needs to be done, rather than avoiding this opportunity out of fear of corruption.

Setting a prisoner minimum wage lower than regular minimum wage is not what it seems because prisoners are already being provided necessities of life. The average cost per inmate is $33,274, with a low of $14,780 in Alabama and a high of $69,355 in New York (Mai). Prisoners are provided with necessities of life like housing and food, costs that would drain an unincarcerated person’s paycheck. Low wages are acceptable in prisons because prisons are also paying the prisoner in food and shelter. With the implementation of prison labor with lower wages should come upgrades on food and bed quality, as work is meant to be an exchange. The prisoners provide the work and the prison prides compensation through money, food, and housing. Diane Dimond, a journalist for the Huffington Post, brings into the light some ways in which certain states are saving money by requiring their prisoners to work, slowly decreasing our national debt. She says that in New York, “the state is saving about three and a half million dollars each year by having inmates man the Call Center phones for the Department of Motor Vehicles...The inmates find purpose and the states save money” (Dimond). In the article, Diamond also makes the point that although inmates are getting paid the very minimum, “on average it costs roughly $29,000 a year to keep an inmate locked up” (Dimond). Recognizing the money spent on each prisoner is important in deciding their minimum wage. Paying prisoners allows them to buy phone calls or good soap and have some money saved up for when they are released from prison, but keeping that wage low will allow the government to save money and entice outside companies to participate; all parties benefit. Although reducing prison costs would ideally stem from having fewer prisoners, the way to have fewer prisoners is through reducing recidivism and mass incarceration. Prison labor will be able to do that, eventually reducing the cost of prisons overall.

Will Prison Labor Take Jobs Away From Law-Abiding Citizens?

While using outside companies for prison labor is important, a concern is that prison labor will take jobs away from law-abiding citizens. This is a valid concern as there is a struggle for citizens without criminal records to find jobs and the requirement of prison labor poses a threat to employment opportunities. With this concern in mind, the maximum number of jobs will be found inside prisons and jobs connected to outside businesses will be available to unemployed Americans at minimum wage. Using outside businesses is necessary to give jobs to all prisoners. Unemployment in America is an issue, but the unemployment of formerly incarcerated people is even more of a concern. A report released in July 2018 by the Prison Policy Initiative shows that 27 percent of an estimated five million ex-offenders nationwide, around 1,350,000 people, are unemployed. In perspective, a prisoner’s unemployment rate is “higher than the total U.S. unemployment rate during any historical period, including the Great Depression” (Horn). The general population’s unemployment rate is only four percent, making ex-offenders more likely to be unemployed by 6 times (Horn). It is unjust to allow this difference to continue. To reduce an inmate’s unemployment rate and therefore decrease the overall unemployment rate, prison labor is crucial and will require jobs from the current job market. With the best interest of all Americans, these prison labor jobs will also be available to unemployed Americans who can not find a job elsewhere.

Is Prison Labor Slavery?

Although the benefits and logistics of prison labor have been shown, many may still be concerned about the implications of prison labor as a form of slavery that will lead to exploitation. The discriminatory and slave history of our country should cause concern when dealing with this topic. Our country is overcoming exploitive slavery and aspects of discrimination, but as these issues are still present it is important to be aware and cautious. The United States needs to be able to learn from the past corruption of work through slavery, not avoid it. Unfortunately, our country was corrupt and still faces that corruption, but now we have the chance to prove what we have learned from exploring work and prove the exceptional benefits of work. Required prison labor, although involuntary, is not slavery. Slavery is when individuals are owned by others and seen as property. Slaves are forced to work for nothing (What Is Slavery?). By paying prisoners, treating prisoners as employees rather than property, and targeting work programs for the benefit of the prisoners rather than the companies all separate prison labor from slavery. There will be flaws with required prison work that will be worked through, but without an attempt to make it work, an opportunity will be missed. As stated before, prison labor can be separated from slavery by paying prisoners, applying labor laws similar to those in our current workforce, holding an American-wide standard for prison labor, and seeing prisoners as employees rather than property. With rules, regulations, and awareness of the potential issues, exploitation can be avoided and our corrupt past can be overcome. The 13th Amendment of the U.S. Constitution was passed by Congress on January 31, 1865, to abolish slavery. Section one of this amendment states, “Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted, shall exist within the United States, or any place subject to their jurisdiction.” (Congress). Although requiring prisoners to work is legal according to the U.S. Constitution as a punishment for crime, this program is not a punishment for crime, but a fruitful opportunity for the prisoner’s improvement. The required prison labor program will be able to redefine the motives of prison from retribution and isolation to rehabilitation. Prison labor rehabilitates the prisoner, not the degrade them as slavery does. It is important to be aware of the possibility of exploitation because of the 13th amendment’s exception and communicate the restorative intentions of required prison work.

Prison labor will even counter corruption and violence throughout prisons by keeping prisoners from idleness and boredom. Chandra Bozelko states counter-arguments of prison labor, arguing it to be a form of slavery, are “unfair and counterproductive” because prisoners “couldn’t wait to head to work.” Most prisoners are simply bored and others want to get away from the violence throughout the prison. These hard conditions throughout prisons including riots and crime have a direct correlation to the idleness of prisoners. Bozelko witnessed prisoners who became hostile when “they were not making use of their time” because “they were bored”. Requiring prisoners to work lowers the idleness of prisoners, keeping them happy and decreasing violence throughout prisons.

The concept behind slavery is a corruption of a human’s freedom, but Bozelko claims from first-hand experience that “when a prisoner is working, she is the closest to free she can be” (Bozelko). Countless measures need to be taken to ensure prison labor is not exploited, but this is an incredible opportunity for prisoners to feel free in a place of required routine. Bozelko is not the only one who admits this. As mentioned earlier, Jesse Luke Crosson felt working with tools showed there was “a trust in [his] person and in [his] competence, which gives [him] validation” (Crosson) and Pennsylvania inmates participating in PCI had increased feelings of “metaphorical “freedom” from prison” (Littletree-Holston). The work showed Crosson that he could be trusted and have a purpose. The comparison of prison labor to slavery is unfair because there is a difference between the motive of slave owners and prisons; slave owners used labor to show slaves as property and decrease their sense of humanity, but prison labor is working to restore a prisoner’s sense of humanity. Prisoners are excited about the opportunity and are excited to find freedom.

This system of work in prisons will redefine what it means to require work; required work will be an opportunity for self-improvement and a second chance.

As a country that promotes freedom and opportunity, we should strive to bring this opportunity to all people and give them this second chance. Although these prisoners have directly gone against our law, they still deserve to live the American dream. Implementation will not be the end; prison labor will have multiple problems and bring about big picture discussions. These problems need to be worked through and the discussions need to be had. The Christian Perspective

The Christian perspective offers a helpful explanation of work, stemming from human nature according to the Bible. One may see work as a hard task often required and easily corrupted. This causes them to see work as a product of the fall, but the Bible reveals that God designed humans to work. In Genesis 2:15, “The Lord God took the man and put him in the garden of Eden to work it and keep it” (New International Version). Before the fall when Adam was first created, God designated him to work. Sin has corrupted the pure intention of work as seen through slavery, but it is human nature to work. Giving prisoners the opportunity to work is a gift just as God intended work to be; it revitalizes their humanity.
